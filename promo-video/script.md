# River Bankers — Promo Video Script

**Published:** https://youtu.be/9Q7okytYiio (2026-06-08)


## Format

Each scene block is `[start–end] (visual cue) Narration text.` Times are in
seconds. The TTS engine produces one MP3 per scene; Remotion uses `<Audio>`
+ `<Sequence>` to align cues. Visual cues are notes for the animator — they
do not appear in the narration.

**Source of truth for game rules:** `rulebook/rulebook.html`. The auction
walkthrough below is written to match the rulebook's "Bidding" and
"Resolving the auction" sections exactly — *simultaneous blind bid*, *pay
fish first*, *plenty vs. jam*, *card moves only after the auction settles*.

## Full 2-minute promo (target: 120s, ~300 words at ~155 wpm)

The auction segment (0:32–1:30) is the centerpiece — a full minute walking
through one concrete Headwaters auction, then contrasting plenty (bids ≤
icons) with a jam (bids > icons, with the overbid math). Lapping gets a
one-sentence aside; recall is one sentence too; everything else is just
long enough to register.

### S1 — Hook (0:00–0:10, ~10s)

```
[00.0–10.0] (Full-board reveal with all four species pawns visible on the
            fish track. Quick zoom on each species token as it's named, then
            pull back to a wide static shot.)
            Meet the River Bankers: Beavers, otters, muskrats, minks.
            Four rival groups of animals fighting to collect resources
            and build structures along the riverbank.
```

### S2 — Setup / board geography (0:10–0:22, ~12s)

```
[10.0–22.0] (Camera pan left→right across the board: Material Deck →
            Headwaters 3→2→1 → River 1→2→3→4 → Shoreline. As each material
            is named, that icon pulses on a nearby card.)
            Cards drift from the headwaters through four river spaces.
            Each shows a row of icons - logs, stones, reeds, mud, vines,
            or clay.
```

### S3 — Turn structure / fish economy (0:22–0:32, ~10s)

```
[22.0–32.0] (Cut to the fish track. The pawn furthest back glows; an arrow
            pulses on it. Brief float of fish-cost icons over the four
            actions on the board.)
            On your turn, you take one action and pay its cost in fish.
            Whoever sits furthest back on the fish track acts next.
```

### S4 — Auction walkthrough (0:32–1:30, ~58s) — *the centerpiece*

```
[32.0–37.0] (Reset to a clean board with Reed Stand in Headwaters 1
            (5 reed icons), other cards visible in the other slots.
            Cut-in label "MULTI-UNIT AUCTION".)
            The main action is a multi-unit auction — the kind PhDs
            designed for FCC spectrum sales. Watch.

[37.0–46.0] (Beaver pawn glows. Reed Stand stays in HW1 with a yellow
            highlight ring. Beaver pays 2🐟 — pawn slides +2 on the fish
            track. Privacy screens flip up in front of each player; each
            player picks a bid dial; numbers stay hidden. Caption strip:
            "secretly bid 0 – 5 workers".)
            I pay 2 fish to auction this Reed Stand. The card stays put
            while we bid. Everyone picks secretly — anywhere from zero
            up to the five uncovered icons.

[46.0–53.0] (Privacy screens drop simultaneously. Big bid reveal:
            "BEAVER 3 · OTTER 1". Both species' icons pulse.)
            Bids are revealed at the same time. I bid three. The otter
            bids one.

[53.0–64.0] (Caption "PAY FISH FIRST · 1🐟 per icon". Three fish icons
            stream off the beaver supply; one off the otter supply.
            Beaver pawn slides +3 on the fish track; otter pawn +1.)
            Pay fish first — at the Headwaters that's 1 fish per icon,
            paid on your full bid. I spend 3 more; the otter, 1.

[64.0–74.0] (Beaver chits drop onto 3 reed icons (top row); otter chit
            drops onto 1 reed icon (bottom row). Big caption: "PLENTY
            · bids ≤ icons · everyone gets what they bid". One reed icon
            still uncovered with a faint pulse.)
            Then place workers. Combined we bid four — under the card's
            five icons. That's "plenty": everyone gets exactly what they
            bid.

[74.0–86.0] (Rewind effect — workers fly back off the card. Replay with
            bids set to 4 (beaver) and 3 (otter). Bid reveal:
            "BEAVER 4 · OTTER 3". Big caption: "JAM · bids > icons ·
            overbid = 2". Beaver ends with 2 workers placed (2 returned
            to supply); otter ends with 1 worker placed (2 returned to
            supply). Fish track advances by 4+3.)
            But suppose I bid four and the otter three. Seven workers,
            but only five icons on the card: that's an overbid of two.
            Subtract that from each bid: I take two, the otter takes one.
            The extras go home empty, but the fish are gone.

[86.0–96.0] (Back to the original plenty outcome. The Reed Stand slides
            from Headwaters 1 down into River 1. Cost ribbon morphs from
            "1🐟 per icon" to "2🐟 per icon". As the price morphs, a
            subtle caption tags the moment: "your choices shape the
            prices". Camera briefly pulls back to show the whole river
            — every card sits where players left it, every cost
            reflects past choices.)
            Uncovered icons left? The card drifts one slot downstream —
            into costlier water. Cards only move when players auction
            them, so what you auction shapes the prices everyone faces.
```

### Contest alignment — "player-driven value"

Contest brief: "Systems where values change automatically (such as
increasing costs each round) without meaningful player influence do not
meet that requirement."

#### The auction mechanism

River Bankers adapts a **multi-unit auction** to a tabletop game. The
underlying algorithm is the same family PhD economists designed for
FCC spectrum sales: bidders compete for *quantities of identical items*
on a single lot (a card's icons), not for single objects. When demand
fits supply, everyone gets exactly what they bid; when demand exceeds
supply (a "jam"), items are allocated by `take = max(0, bid − overbid)`
and the price for the *remaining* uncovered items escalates in future
rounds.

#### Why it implements "player-driven value"

River Bankers' per-item cost gradient (1🐟 at the Headwaters → 5🐟 at
River 4) *looks* like an automatic round-tick — but the actual rule is
that **a card only moves when a player auctions it**. There is no round
timer. Every drift, every price escalation, every uncovered icon you
have to pay more for, exists because somebody chose to auction it.
Auctioning is itself a price-setting move: the active player accepts a
known per-item cost now in exchange for leaving a costlier card for
whoever bids on it next. Two visible meters — workers in front of you,
fish track across the board — make every bid a transparent, bounded
commitment: you can't accidentally overspend, you can't surprise-
bankrupt, you can't run out of change.

#### Theme-integration angle

The theme isn't decoration on top of the math; the geography *is* the
math.
- **Animal workers physically fetch** the resources they bid on:
  bidding tokens and worker tokens are the same physical objects, on
  the same card.
- **"Jams" really jam**: demand-exceeds-supply is modelled as a literal
  river jam — too many critters chasing too few items.
- **Drift = cost escalation**: a card that doesn't fully clear floats
  one space downstream into a costlier slot. The cost gradient on the
  board itself implements the price-escalation rule; players don't have
  to remember any modifier.

The result is a deceptively sophisticated auction that *feels* like a
worker-placement game in play. The rules feel obvious because the
physical river enforces them.

> *River Bankers adapts a multi-unit auction mechanism — the kind
> economists designed to sell FCC wireless spectrum — into a worker-
> placement tabletop game. There is no automatic round timer; every
> per-item cost in the river is the consequence of a specific player
