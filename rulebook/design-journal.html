River Bankers — Design Journal

Week-by-week development log · oldest first

This is the story of how River Bankers came together, told a week at a time. Where the Release Notes are a changelog for players, this journal is for the design record: what we were trying to fix, what we were trying to feel, and why each mechanic landed where it did. The through-line is a single question we kept re-asking — how do you price a bid when there's no money on the table?

River Bankers began life on [2026-05-04] as a retheme of an earlier auction design — turning the money players used to pay into a shared fish track, and the bidding tokens into species-themed worker meeples. Focused development began the next day; that's where this journal picks up.

Phase I · Core engine (May)

Week of May 5 — the auction skeleton

The founding idea landed fully formed: there is no money. Instead of paying dollars to a bank, players spend fish by advancing a pawn along a shared track, and turn order is the track — whoever's pawn sits farthest back acts next. Cost never gates a bid; it only buries you deeper in the turn queue. Workers do double duty as both bid tokens and inventory: place them on a material card to win icons, and they sit there until you spend them building.

The core tension we were chasing: every bid should hurt in time, not cash. A big grab is powerful but sends you to the back of the line, so several opponents act before you do again. Patience — small bids that keep your pawn forward — buys you tempo. The whole economy is a bet on when you'd rather act.

By the end of the first week the spine was in place: a pre-river auction feeding a four-slot river, slot-based pricing (cards carry no printed cost — fish is paid by where a card sits), and the first playable digital prototype. Two early problems got their first fixes:

  • Jammed auctions. When demand outstripped supply, a card could ping-pong down the river with nobody ever winning. We introduced pay-for-bid in jams: every worker you commit costs fish even if it doesn't clinch an icon. This one rule later proved to cut wasted auctions ~35× in simulation.
  • Endless games. Added a looping track with lap-exhaustion (a pawn lapped a full loop flips over and sits out until the leader passes again) plus a Flush action and a first-pass endgame phase, so games could actually end.

We also grew the raw economy: the deck was shrunk to a tidy 5 × [4–8] icon spread, and — crucially — we wrote a simulator that could play thousands of games against itself, giving us hard data on balance instead of guesswork. A sixth material (clay) replaced an awkward "fish material," giving three natural wildcard pairs. The pre-river queue got its lasting name — Headwaters — and the time track became the Fish Track.

Structure effects, in three phases the build side

Materials are only half the game; the other half is building. We designed the structure-card effects in three waves — passive economy boosts, on-build one-shots, and reactive triggers — and taught the AI opponents to actually use those effects, so the simulated games would reflect real play. From here on, our main balancing method was to test each card by measuring how much it changed the outcome — play with it, play without it, and see how the win rates moved.

Week of May 12 — quiet week

One structural fix: when the structure deck empties, its discard reshuffles back in, so the build economy can't dead-end in a long game.

Week of May 19 — asymmetry & card identity

This week River Bankers stopped being a symmetric resource race and grew a species identity.

Problem: four near-identical players making the same optimal moves is a solved, flat game. We wanted beavers to feel like dam-builders and otters like nimble opportunists without bolting on a rules exception per species.

Solution: asymmetric starter drafts. Each species draws one of three themed starter structures at setup, giving an opening personality without touching the core loop. We then spent real effort balancing the starters by printed VP (and a Stone Tool nerf) so no species opened ahead.

Alongside that:

  • Eight effect-bearing material cards were folded into existing deck slots — wilds and small triggers — so the material half gained texture without inflating the deck.
  • Four "novel-action" structures (Tribute Stone, Beaver Tow, Otter Trail, Salmon Run) added cards that do something on your turn rather than just scoring.
  • Vine Curtain — peek at the top two of a deck and rearrange — shipped for both AI and human, our first hidden-information lever.
  • Deferred bids (Spy Mound / Quick Strike: react to others' bids) got their logic fixed, and several rebalance passes pushed end-game-effect cards toward printed VP 0 so their power lived in the effect, not double-dipped into score.

Week of May 26 — components, art & the VP model

A production-minded week. We locked worker tokens at 0.63" discs in four species colors, named every material card, and moved the decks from placeholder text to real designed card art shown in the playable prototype. "Sea Otter" became River Otter throughout.

Problem our balance testing kept surfacing: a middle tier of 7-VP cards was crowding the decision space — never clearly worth it, never clearly not. We eliminated the 7-VP tier and bumped under-priced 0-VP cards, sharpening every build into a real choice.

Two lasting scoring/endgame ideas arrived:

  • End-of-game pair-VP for unspent worker pairs — leftover workers aren't dead weight, they convert to points, so hoarding has an exit value.
  • Flush now shuffles Headwaters cards back into the deck rather than discarding them outright, keeping the deck honest.

The physical footprint firmed up too: a generated river-board mat (Headwaters row above a four-wide river), a tier badge on material cards marking which join at 3+ and 4 players, and a small-but-real balance tweak — 4-player games start with 7 workers, not 8, to tighten early capacity when the table is crowded.

Phase II · Production & the endgame problem (June)

Week of June 2 — the box, the board, and a false start

Physical production in earnest: the 60-cell perimeter fish board with an integrated structure deck/discard and Invent action, box lid and bottom artwork for the TGC retail box, printed worker chits, and the first real rulebook (drafts v0.1.1 → v0.1.6). Four new structures (Trophy Lodge, Springwater Pool, Spring Cascade, Trading Post) rounded out the deck, and effect text was standardized across every card.

An experiment we reverted. We tried having the endgame's entry fire all the "at 0 fish / pass-0" freebie effects at once. In testing it distorted the final turns — a burst of free value that rewarded whoever happened to hold those cards rather than good play. We reverted it the same week and put more rigorous balance testing in place for the next endgame idea. This was the first clear signal that the endgame itself — not the cards — was the real problem.

Week of June 9 — into the contest

Milestone — contest entry. On [2026-06-09] River Bankers was entered into the TGC "Everything Has a Price" contest, the finish line the whole project had been aimed at. The entry followed a TGC community-verified test that scored 118 / 120 — outside validation that the game held together before it went in.

With the prototype ordered and the game entered, attention turned to presentation (TGC shop assets, a promo video) and one meaningful new mechanic:

  • Confluence — pay fish to run a single auction across two material cards of the same item type, pooling their icons into one larger contested grab. It gives players a way to force a big fight instead of waiting for supply to stack up naturally.
  • Double Vision shipped as an experimental optional rule (combined placement, clockwise), and the AI was taught to reach the Beaver Tow / Salmon Run actions it had been ignoring — an AI blind spot that had been quietly skewing balance data.

Week of June 16 — the endgame overhaul

The headline of the whole project — and it was driven by the game's first in-person playtest. On [2026-06-16] the physical prototype hit the table with friends for the first time (every prior playtest had been against the computer in the digital prototype), and the human table reported the same feeling the sim never could: the game should wrap up about when the material deck ran dry, but the old "snap all pawns to 1, 2, 3… then grind to space 59" ending dragged well past that point. The overhaul landed the very next day.

The redesign: end on a finish line, not on the deck. Players race toward a fish-track target; crossing it retires you; when all have retired everyone takes one last build, then scores. The deck running out no longer ends the game — but once it's empty the active player drifts +1 fish per turn so the dead-board tail rushes to a close instead of grinding.

The rework cascaded into several supporting changes:

  • Lapping / exhaustion was removed entirely — with a hard finish line you pass 0 at most once or twice, so the lap-out safety net became dead weight. Pawns now just flip to a "+60" side after a lap so their true position stays readable.
  • Fish lines were set to 59 / 89 / 119 (2P/3P/4P) — tuned so a single printed "+60" chit exactly covers the second lap.
  • Five "pass-0" cards were reworked. The new endgame capped laps, which gutted cards that could only fire "at 0 on the fish track" — they'd get one shot at the very end. We converted them to once-per-game abilities. Mill Wheel's old board-clamp would have actively blocked a player from finishing, so it was rebuilt as a copy-your-neighbour card.

Min-Invent, and other race-tuning

Problem the finish line exposed: a player with nothing to build could sit at Invent-for-1, drawing a card and inching forward one space at a time — a slow "draw one, inch forward" grind right where the game most wants to end.

Fix: Invent now costs at least 2 fish (choose N = 2–5, was 1–5). A stuck player advances to the line faster without changing how much anyone actually builds. The same week added a player-facing strategy guide, made Cache Burrow's hand-size bonus stack, gave every species its own card back, reconciled a batch of species-named cards between docs and print, and taught Heron Roost to reshuffle its displaced card back into the deck (matching Flush's recycle rule). Starting player is now randomized with a documented first-player rule and a fairness audit.

Week of June 23 — recalibration, then BoardGameArena

Two 4-player playtests drove a final endgame recalibration:

  • The fish line moved from the climbing 89/109/119 to a uniform 119, then — after more data — to a flat 90 at every player count, paired with the deck-empty +1 drift and a fix so no two pawns share a finish space. Flat-90 landed games at roughly 30 / 40 / 55 minutes (2P/3P/4P).
  • An AI over-bidding fix plus worker/endgame recalibration cleaned up the simulation baseline the balance data rides on.
  • A uniform rule was written down and enforced everywhere: a card's own effects never apply to the build that creates it. This killed a class of self-referential edge cases that had been handled inconsistently.
Bringing it to the table online. Mid-week we began building a version of the game for BoardGameArena, so it could be played online against real opponents. Beyond reaching more players, this rebuild earned its keep as a rules auditor: re-creating every card effect from a blank slate forced us to pin down ambiguities the original prototype had let slide.

Smaller rules refinements landed alongside: a Pier "(max +6)" cap, and pre-auction worker recall from shoreline cards folded cleanly into the bid step.

Week of June 30 — Alpha at BGA, clarity pass & the 2-player deck

Milestone — BoardGameArena Alpha. On [2026-06-30], with the full game ported and playable online, we requested Alpha review status on BoardGameArena — the step that opens the game to real online opponents beyond the design table.

Preparing for that review, the online build surfaced a set of rules questions that we answered consistently across every version of the game — the online play, the prototype, and the rulebook all kept in step:

  • Confluence effect text nailed down to the min-cost rule.
  • Final builds skip "when built" effects — the last-build coda shouldn't trigger a fresh cascade of on-build value.
  • The river-card "Auction" action was renamed "Swim" to stop colliding with the generic auction concept.
  • Mill Wheel's copy now picks the most beneficial effect, and Slipstream was pulled from the BGA port — its "take a turn immediately after another player" queue-jump has no clean realization in BGA's turn model.

A card designed for two players the last mechanic

Problem: at 2 players the material market flattens — with only two bidders, mid-game contention thins out and two of the wildcard-less materials (stones, vines) rarely fight.

Bramble Shoal is a 2-player-only card: at 2P the two vanilla 5-icon cards on stones and vines (Rocky Shoal, Trailing Vine) come out and are replaced by a single shared stones/vines wild, manufacturing contention at exactly the count where the market goes quiet — and completing the symmetric set of three wildcard pairs (logs/reeds, clay/mud, stones/vines). It ships with a green "2P" badge; its two displaced cards now carry matching "3+" badges so players know to pull them at two players. The auction engine also learned to support multiple simultaneous Spy Mound / Quick Strike deferrers, matching the BGA build.
