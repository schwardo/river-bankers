River Bankers — Design Journal

Week-by-week development log · oldest first

This is the story of how River Bankers came together, told a week at a time. Where the Release Notes are a changelog for players, this journal is for the design record: what we were trying to fix, what we were trying to feel, and why each mechanic landed where it did. The through-line is a single question we kept re-asking — how do you price a bid when there's no money on the table?

Phase 0 · Origins (late April)

Week of April 28 where the auction came from

River Bankers didn't start as a river, or even as a game about building. Its core idea came from an earlier project I never finished: Filament, a nanotech game themed around 3-D printers building matter atom by atom. Filament as a whole didn't come together, but one part of it did: a multi-item auction I had already built and playtested. It put several items up at once and let players bid across them, with the tension coming from who commits to what. That part worked well enough to justify a game built around just that mechanic.

Around that time, The Game Crafter announced the “Everything Has a Price” contest, an auction-design challenge. It was a good fit for the part of Filament that had been working, so I decided to enter. This time I wanted to keep the scope tight. Instead of carrying a whole game's worth of subsystems, I would slim everything down to feature the one mechanic I had already tested and build the smallest game around it.

The piece I still needed was cost. An auction needs a price, and I didn't want to use money, since money makes bidding a matter of arithmetic. What worked was pairing the auction with a time track. You don't pay dollars, you pay position, and because the track is shared, a large bid moves you past your opponents. You can overspend, and overspending costs you tempo instead of cash. With those two pieces, a multi-item auction and a track you can overrun, the design had enough to build on, and I started looking for a theme.

Where it started. Two of River Bankers' three pillars were set before the theme existed: the multi-item auction (later, bidding workers across a card's icons) and paying in time rather than money (later, the fish track). The weeks that follow are mostly about finding the game that best fits those two ideas.

On [2026-05-04] the theme arrived: a riverbank of beavers, otters, muskrats, and mink. The money became a shared fish track, the bidding tokens became species-themed worker meeples, and focused development began the next day, where the rest of this journal picks up.

Phase I · Core engine (May)

Week of May 5 — the auction skeleton

The week before had settled the two core ideas; this week turned them into something playable. Turn order became the track itself: whoever's pawn sits farthest back acts next, so a bid is never blocked by its cost, it only pushes you deeper into the queue. A big grab is still powerful, but it sends you to the back of the line while several opponents act, so keeping small and forward buys you tempo. Workers did double duty as bid tokens and inventory, sitting on a material card once they win its icons until you spend them building.

By the end of the first week the spine was in place: a pre-river auction feeding a four-slot river, slot-based pricing (cards carry no printed cost — fish is paid by where a card sits), and the first playable digital prototype. Two early problems got their first fixes:

  • Jammed auctions. When demand outstripped supply, a card could ping-pong down the river with nobody ever winning. We introduced pay-for-bid in jams: every worker you commit costs fish even if it doesn't clinch an icon. This one rule later proved to cut wasted auctions ~35× in simulation.
  • Endless games. Added a looping track with lap-exhaustion (a pawn lapped a full loop flips over and sits out until the leader passes again) plus a Flush action and a first-pass endgame phase, so games could actually end.

We also grew the raw economy: the deck was shrunk to a tidy 5 × [4–8] icon spread, and — crucially — we wrote a simulator that could play thousands of games against itself, giving us hard data on balance instead of guesswork. A sixth material (clay) replaced an awkward "fish material," giving three natural wildcard pairs. The pre-river queue also got its lasting name this week: Headwaters.

Structure effects, in three phases the build side

Materials are only half the game; the other half is building. We designed the structure-card effects in three waves — passive economy boosts, on-build one-shots, and reactive triggers — and taught the AI opponents to actually use those effects, so the simulated games would reflect real play. From here on, our main balancing method was to test each card by measuring how much it changed the outcome — play with it, play without it, and see how the win rates moved.

Week of May 12 — quiet week

One structural fix: when the structure deck empties, its discard reshuffles back in, so the build economy can't dead-end in a long game.

Week of May 19 — asymmetry & card identity

This week River Bankers stopped being a symmetric resource race and grew a species identity.

Problem: four near-identical players making the same optimal moves is a solved, flat game. We wanted beavers to feel like dam-builders and otters like nimble opportunists without bolting on a rules exception per species.

Solution: asymmetric starter drafts. Each species draws one of three themed starter structures at setup, giving an opening personality without touching the core loop. We then spent real effort balancing the starters by printed VP (and a Stone Tool nerf) so no species opened ahead.

Alongside that:

  • Eight effect-bearing material cards were folded into existing deck slots — wilds and small triggers — so the material half gained texture without inflating the deck.
  • Four "novel-action" structures (Tribute Stone, Beaver Tow, Otter Trail, Salmon Run) added cards that do something on your turn rather than just scoring.
  • Vine Curtain — peek at the top two of a deck and rearrange — shipped for both AI and human, our first hidden-information lever.
  • Deferred bids (Spy Mound / Quick Strike: react to others' bids) got their logic fixed, and several rebalance passes pushed end-game-effect cards toward printed VP 0 so their power lived in the effect, not double-dipped into score.

Week of May 26 — components, art & the VP model

A production-minded week. We locked worker tokens at 0.63" discs in four species colors, named every material card, and moved the decks from placeholder text to real designed card art shown in the playable prototype. "Sea Otter" became River Otter throughout.

Problem our balance testing kept surfacing: a middle tier of 7-VP cards was crowding the decision space — never clearly worth it, never clearly not. We eliminated the 7-VP tier and bumped under-priced 0-VP cards, sharpening every build into a real choice.

Two lasting scoring/endgame ideas arrived:

  • End-of-game pair-VP for unspent worker pairs — leftover workers aren't dead weight, they convert to points, so hoarding has an exit value.
  • Flush now shuffles Headwaters cards back into the deck rather than discarding them outright, keeping the deck honest.

The physical footprint firmed up too: a generated river-board mat (Headwaters row above a four-wide river), a tier badge on material cards marking which join at 3+ and 4 players, and a small-but-real balance tweak — 4-player games start with 7 workers, not 8, to tighten early capacity when the table is crowded. (Reverted in late June once the AI over-bidding fix removed the jams that motivated it — see the week of June 23.)

Phase II · Production & the endgame problem (June)

Week of June 2 — the box, the board, and a false start

Physical production in earnest: the 60-cell perimeter fish board with an integrated structure deck/discard and Invent action, box lid and bottom artwork for the TGC retail box, printed worker chits, and the first real rulebook (drafts v0.1.1 → v0.1.6). Four new structures (Trophy Lodge, Springwater Pool, Spring Cascade, Trading Post) rounded out the deck, and effect text was standardized across every card.

An experiment we reverted. We tried having the endgame's entry fire all the "at 0 fish / pass-0" freebie effects at once. In testing it distorted the final turns — a burst of free value that rewarded whoever happened to hold those cards rather than good play. We reverted it the same week and put more rigorous balance testing in place for the next endgame idea. This was the first clear signal that the endgame itself — not the cards — was the real problem.

Week of June 9 — into the contest

Milestone — contest entry. On [2026-06-09] River Bankers was entered into the TGC "Everything Has a Price" contest, the finish line the whole project had been aimed at. The entry followed a TGC community-verified test that scored 118 / 120 — outside validation that the game held together before it went in.

With the prototype ordered and the game entered, attention turned to presentation (TGC shop assets, a promo video) and one meaningful new mechanic:

  • Confluence — pay fish to run a single auction across two material cards of the same item type, pooling their icons into one larger contested grab. It gives players a way to force a big fight instead of waiting for supply to stack up naturally.
  • Double Vision shipped as an experimental optional rule (combined placement, clockwise), and the AI was taught to reach the Beaver Tow / Salmon Run actions it had been ignoring — an AI blind spot that had been quietly skewing balance data.

Week of June 16 — the endgame overhaul

The headline of the whole project — and it was driven by the game's first in-person playtest. On [2026-06-16] the physical prototype hit the table with friends for the first time (every prior playtest had been against the computer in the digital prototype), and the human table reported the same feeling the sim never could: the game should wrap up about when the material deck ran dry, but the old "snap all pawns to 1, 2, 3… then grind to space 59" ending dragged well past that point. The overhaul landed the very next day.

The redesign: end on a finish line, not on the deck. Players race toward a fish-track target; crossing it retires you; when all have retired everyone takes one last build, then scores. The deck running out no longer ends the game — but once it's empty the active player drifts +1 fish per turn so the dead-board tail rushes to a close instead of grinding.

The rework cascaded into several supporting changes:

  • Lapping / exhaustion was removed entirely — with a hard finish line you pass 0 at most once or twice, so the lap-out safety net became dead weight. Pawns now just flip to a "+60" side after a lap so their true position stays readable.
  • Fish lines were set to 59 / 89 / 119 (2P/3P/4P) — tuned so a single printed "+60" chit exactly covers the second lap.
  • Five "pass-0" cards were reworked. The new endgame capped laps, which gutted cards that could only fire "at 0 on the fish track" — they'd get one shot at the very end. We converted them to once-per-game abilities. Mill Wheel's old board-clamp would have actively blocked a player from finishing, so it was rebuilt as a copy-your-neighbour card.

Min-Invent, and other race-tuning

Problem the finish line exposed: a player with nothing to build could sit at Invent-for-1, drawing a card and inching forward one space at a time — a slow "draw one, inch forward" grind right where the game most wants to end.

Fix: Invent now costs at least 2 fish (choose N = 2–5, was 1–5). A stuck player advances to the line faster without changing how much anyone actually builds. The same week added a player-facing strategy guide, made Cache Burrow's hand-size bonus stack, gave every species its own card back, reconciled a batch of species-named cards between docs and print, and taught Heron Roost to reshuffle its displaced card back into the deck (matching Flush's recycle rule). Starting player is now randomized with a documented first-player rule and a fairness audit.

Week of June 23 — recalibration, then BoardGameArena

Two 4-player playtests drove a final endgame recalibration:

  • The fish line moved from a uniform 119 to a climbing 89/109/119, then — once the AI over-bidding fix below cleaned up the data — to a flat 90 at every player count, paired with the deck-empty +1 drift and a fix so no two pawns share a finish space. Flat-90 landed games at roughly 30 / 40 / 55 minutes (2P/3P/4P). Why one line works at every count: a larger board at more players takes longer to mine out, but the extra workers and richer auctions carry every pawn to the line at about the same depth — so a single flat line ends all counts organically, by pawns crossing, rather than stalling on a depleted board.
  • An AI over-bidding fix plus worker/endgame recalibration cleaned up the simulation baseline the balance data rides on — and reversed May's 4-player worker trim: every player count now starts with the full 8 workers.
  • A uniform rule was written down and enforced everywhere: a card's own effects never apply to the build that creates it. This killed a class of self-referential edge cases that had been handled inconsistently.
Bringing it to the table online. Mid-week we began building a version of the game for BoardGameArena, so it could be played online against real opponents. Beyond reaching more players, this rebuild earned its keep as a rules auditor: re-creating every card effect from a blank slate forced us to pin down ambiguities the original prototype had let slide.

Smaller rules refinements landed alongside: a Pier "(max +6)" cap, and pre-auction worker recall from shoreline cards folded cleanly into the bid step.

Week of June 30 — Alpha at BGA, clarity pass & the 2-player deck

Milestone — BoardGameArena Alpha. On [2026-06-30], with the full game ported and playable online, we requested Alpha review status on BoardGameArena — the step that opens the game to real online opponents beyond the design table.

Preparing for that review, the online build surfaced a set of rules questions that we answered consistently across every version of the game — the online play, the prototype, and the rulebook all kept in step:

  • Confluence effect text nailed down to the min-cost rule.
  • Final builds skip "when built" effects — the last-build coda shouldn't trigger a fresh cascade of on-build value.
  • The river-card "Auction" action was renamed "Swim" to stop colliding with the generic auction concept.
  • Mill Wheel's copy now picks the most beneficial effect, and Slipstream was pulled from the BGA port — its "take a turn immediately after another player" queue-jump has no clean realization in BGA's turn model.

A card designed for two players the last mechanic

Problem: at 2 players the material market flattens — with only two bidders, mid-game contention thins out and two of the wildcard-less materials (stones, vines) rarely fight.

Bramble Shoal is a 2-player-only card: at 2P the two vanilla 5-icon cards on stones and vines (Rocky Shoal, Trailing Vine) come out and are replaced by a single shared stones/vines wild, manufacturing contention at exactly the count where the market goes quiet — and completing the symmetric set of three wildcard pairs (logs/reeds, clay/mud, stones/vines). It ships with a green "2P" badge; its two displaced cards now carry matching "3+" badges so players know to pull them at two players. The auction engine also learned to support multiple simultaneous Spy Mound / Quick Strike deferrers, matching the BGA build.

Phase III · Polish for print (July)

Week of June 30 — v0.2: a sanity-test polish pass

Outside eyes. On [2026-07-03] River Bankers came back from The Game Crafter’s Sanity Test — an independent review of the rulebook and shop listing — with a score of 77/100 (“an auction game with a charming theme and professional presentation”, reviewed by Chris Aylott). It praised the writing, setup, and layout, and handed us a punch-list of concrete fixes. Everything in v0.2 is a response to that review (cross-checked against two rulebook-writing guides — University XP’s What Makes a Good Rule Book and The Indie Game Report’s Rulebook Cookbook), so the game got sharper on the page without the rules changing underneath.

The review scored Rules 66/80 and Shop Presentation 11/20, and the feedback clustered into a few themes: add navigation (a table of contents), orient the reader with a summary of play up front, lean on more diagrams, and shore up the shop listing. The changes below work through that list.

Fixes shipped so far in response to the sanity test

  • The sideways auction chit. The reviewer caught that the auction-chit picture “seems to be sideways.” It was: the chit reuses the material-deck-back river scene, which is deliberately rotated to fill the tall 1×2″ token — so on the page it read as a turned-on-its-side landscape. In the rulebook it now shows upright and is sized to scale against the material card beside it.
  • Labeled card anatomy. The review wanted more diagrams and a component-anatomy treatment (echoing both writing guides). The rulebook now has numbered-callout diagrams naming the parts of a structure card and of a material card — the kind of “what am I looking at” reference a first-time reader reaches for.
  • Icon legend. A key for the four worker chits and six material icons now sits in the Quick reference, so the symbols in the tables and on the cards are all decodable in one place.
  • Every component pictured. An audit turned up two components that were described but never shown — the fish-track pawn (now front-and-“+60”-back, so the flip mechanic is visible) and the blank chit. Both had art sitting unused in the repo; they’re now on the page.
  • A summary of play up front. The reviewer wanted the reader oriented with a summary before the detailed “game elements” — the same “game overview” both writing guides call for. A tight How the game plays section now precedes the details: no rounds, one action a turn, fish as cost-and-clock, build for VP, finish-line ending.
  • A thematic cover intro. The cover led with mechanics (“bid your workers on materials… every action costs fish”). Following the Cookbook’s note that a thematic intro should read like advertising copy and never name game terms, it now sets the scene instead — beaver, otter, muskrat, and mink racing to raise the finest works on the riverbank.
  • A “show the final state” setup diagram. Both guides — and the reviewer’s note on setup pictures — ask for a picture of the completed setup. The plain side-by-side board shots are replaced by a single laid-out-table diagram: the material deck and three face-up Headwaters cards, an empty river, the structure deck with all pawns stacked on space 0, and a player’s eight workers, three-card hand, and drafted starter — each labeled and keyed.
  • The endgame stands out. The reviewer found the endgame “gets a little lost on the page” and suggested a separate page or a stronger header — so it got both: its own page, opened by a distinctive banner header that reads at a glance.
  • A worked-auction walkthrough. The reviewer asked for “a couple of visual examples of play.” A three-panel auction — a card comes up, secret bids are revealed, then workers pay and place while the card drifts downstream — now shows the core loop in pictures, and neatly fills the page the endgame’s new page-break had emptied.
  • A table of contents and page numbers. The reviewer’s top structural note — echoed by both guides — was that a rulebook this size wants a table of contents. There’s now a short Contents box up front and a page number on every content page, so any section is a glance away. Inline cross-references were converted from “at the back”/“next page” to real page numbers.
  • Glossary and consistent wording. A short glossary near the back defines the coined terms, and a vocabulary pass settled on one word per thing (both guides warn against alternating synonyms): the fish-track piece is always the pawn, river cards slide, an icon is the circle a worker covers while an item is the abstract unit it yields, and the bidding pieces are worker tokens.
  • Setup builds the deck by its badges. The old “deck size by player count” lookup box is gone; setup now just reads each card’s corner badge — un-badged cards are always in, 3+ cards join at three or four players, 4P cards at four, and the 2P card only at two — so the market is assembled the same way it’s explained.
  • A clarity-and-accuracy pass. A read-through tightened the wording throughout and corrected a handful of imprecise lines — the goal statement, the Headwaters description, what a per-item cost actually charges (each worker you bid, not only the ones that win), and a note that a wildcard worker counts as one of its two materials but never both.
Phase IV · Watching real games (July)

Week of July 6 — the first full table, and a way to watch it

A milestone the whole project had been building toward, quietly: the first game played all the way to scoring on Board Game Arena (in private Alpha) by real, independent opponents — three friends each playing their own seat, not me driving three browser tabs. It reached the finish line, the retire cascade fired, the final builds resolved, and it ranked. Nothing broke. But the more interesting thing is what we could do with it.

Alongside the game we built a small telemetry pipeline: it downloads a finished BGA table's stats and its full move log, then lays that single real game on top of the distribution from four thousand AI-vs-AI games of the simulator. For two years the question about the sim was “does it match the rules?” Now it becomes a sharper one — does real human play match the model we've been balancing against?

The first answer is a clean split: the game predicts outcomes well and process poorly. Where players finished — winner on 25 VP, a 14-point spread from first to last — sits right under the peak of the simulated curve. But how they got there was nothing like the bots. This table had 17 jammed (overbid) auctions against a simulated mean of about five — a game more than three standard deviations more contentious than anything the AI plays. And that one behavior explains all the rest: more jams means fewer clean auctions, fewer icons actually claimed (60 against a typical 85), and a noticeably shorter game.

Back to the founding question. The whole design rests on every bid should hurt in time, not cash — you pay for a grab by falling to the back of the turn queue. These three humans treated that tempo cost as cheap and piled into auction after auction; the fish-frugal AI hangs back and bids clean. So either the bots are under-contesting the market, or real players haven't yet felt how much a jam sets them back — and both readings point at the same lever, the auction bidding heuristic in the sim. That's the first genuinely data-driven balance question the game has raised.

One game is one draw — every figure above is a single table against a distribution, not a trend. The amber marker only becomes a shape of its own once several finished games stack up. But it's the first time real play has talked back to the model, and it had something to say.

Week of July 11 — Alpha ships its first fix back

Last week's entry ended on a promise: now that real games talk back to the model, the model can be corrected. This week that happened for the first time — and it came not from the telemetry distributions but from a player simply noticing something felt unfair. The card in question is Channel Clearer, the muskrat starter that lets you pluck an opponent's worker off a Reed card and hand it back to them. On the tabletop and in the simulator it fires once at the start of your turn. But the Board Game Arena port had quietly implemented it as a repeatable turn-start ability — and for free. In Alpha a muskrat could sweep every opponent Reed worker off the river, every single turn, at no cost. It read as mean, and it played as dominant.

The interesting part is that the simulator had never caught this, because the simulator's own draft AI doesn't want Channel Clearer — it reaches for Mud Burrow every time. So to measure the card at all we had to force the muskrat to run it, then compare four versions of the rule across four thousand games apiece:

BGA: repeatable, free31.1% (+6.1)38.8% (+5.5)

Once/turn, pay 1🐟23.5% (−1.5)32.8% (−0.5)
Once/turn, pay 2🐟22.3% (−2.8)33.5% (+0.1)

The repeatable version is plainly out of band — six points of free win-rate is exactly the “feels unfair” the Alpha player reported, quantified. Capping it at once per turn does the heavy lifting on its own. The question the player actually asked — would charging fish be more balanced? — has a subtler answer: a 1-fish cost isn't dramatically better than free, but it's the steadiest option, the only one inside ±1.5 points at both player counts. And it carries a bonus: the beaver's Tail Slap, the game's only other start-of-turn ability, already costs 1 fish and fires once per turn. Making Channel Clearer match it turns two one-off special cases into a single, learnable rule — start-of-turn abilities cost a fish and fire once.

So v0.3's headline change: Channel Clearer now reads “pay 1 fish to discard 1 worker from any Reeds river card (not a wild card)… once per turn.” The fix went in four places at once — the printed card, the web prototype, the simulator, and the BGA port — because the whole point of this month's work is that those four should never again disagree about what a card does. The first time a discrepancy between them mattered, it cost a muskrat's opponents a fair game; the telemetry pipeline exists so the next one gets caught in software instead of at the table.

Writing the fix surfaced a smaller, sharper question: does “a Reeds card” include Driftwood Tangle, the Logs/Reeds wild? The honest answer is that a wild card has no primary material — its two materials are equal — so it shouldn't count as an exact “Reeds card” at all, and Confluence had already settled this precedent by refusing wilds as same-material pairs. The lesson generalized into a rule for the whole codebase: any effect that tests a card's material must decide, explicitly, whether wilds are in or out — never lean on which of a wild's two materials happens to be printed first. Channel Clearer now excludes them by name, in code and on the card.

Week of July 20 — teaching the model to overbid, and a floor for the initiator

Two threads this week, and they turned out to depend on each other. The first was a nagging credibility problem with the simulator: its default AI is a cautious, worker-hoarding bidder that jams an auction only about 13% of the time, while the humans playing on Board Game Arena jam roughly half their auctions. A model that never overbids can't be trusted to evaluate any rule whose whole purpose is to handle overbidding — so before touching the rules we spent the week making the sim bid more like a person.

That meant two new behavioural knobs rather than a single “play worse” switch. OVERBID makes bidders speculatively pile on a contested card past what pure worker economy would justify; recall reluctance stops them from perfectly recalling and re-spending workers to dodge a jam the way the optimizer does. Bundled together as a “human” profile (OVERBID = 0.5, recall-reluctance = 1.0), they pull the simulated jam rate up out of the cautious-AI basement and into the neighbourhood of what the live telemetry actually shows. It's still a caricature of a real player, but it's a caricature that overbids — which is exactly the regime the next question lived in.

That question: in a bad jam, the player who paid to trigger the auction can walk away with nothing. Because the initiator must always bid at least one worker, and because an auction can be overbid so heavily that no one clinches a single item, you could spend a worker (and its fish) to open a card and get precisely nothing for it. On the tabletop this reads as a feel-bad; in a 3- or 4-player game it happens often enough to matter. The fix is small and, we think, intuitive: a first-mover advantage. When an auction would otherwise leave everyone empty-handed, the initiator still claims one item as a consolation prize. Triggering an auction is never wasted.

With the human profile in place we could finally measure it honestly (off → on):

The 2-player row is the tell: the rule is inert there. With only two capped bidders a total wipe essentially never happens, so the consolation never fires and nothing moves. At 3 and 4 players it's a genuine economic lever — it rescues one to two dead auctions per game, roughly a third more structures get built per player, and (a second-order surprise) the jam rate itself falls by half, because rescued initiators hold different materials and the whole downstream bidding cascade shifts. Games also run longer and score higher, which is the honest cost to weigh: at 45 seconds a turn the 4-player game grows from about 42 to 53 minutes. We shipped it as always-on in the BGA port and the printed rules, and — because the model was finally trustworthy on this exact axis — as an A/B toggle in the web prototype so it can keep being second-guessed. A separate fairness sweep (20k games per config) confirmed it introduces no first-player advantage.
